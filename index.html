College Park Airport

October 1909

Signal Corps selects field in College Park as location for first Army flight training field (Van Allen, 1928)

October 8, 1909

Wilbur Wright begins training Army pilots. (National Park Service)

October 27, 1909

Mrs. Ralph H. Van Deman becomes first woman to be a aircraft passenger. (National Park Service)

November 3, 1909

First Naval officer. Lt. George C. Sweet, to fly a plane. (National Park Service)

June 1911

First flying school established at College Park (Van Allen, 1928)

July 1, 1911

Flying starts at the school (Van Allen, 1928)

August 21, 1911

First flight of over an hour by an Army pilot. Lt. Arnold takes one hour and 2 minutes to cover a distance of 42 miles. (Van Allen, 1928)

October 10, 1911

First experiment using an "intrument of war" conducted when a bomb sight was tested on a plane. (Van Allen, 1928)

November 1912

Plane used for first time in U.S. for experiments in artillery fire control. (Van Allen, 1928)

November 1912

Signal Corps abandons flight school. (National Park Service)

August 12, 1918

First airmail post office in the world established. (Munroe, 1929)

1921

During early 1921 Henry Berliner starts his experiments, which include building the first helicopter to fly both vertically and horizontally (Munroe, 1929)

November 1921

The turkey for President Harding's 1921 Thanksgiving pardon arrives via the College Park Airport (National Air and Space Musuem, 2012)

1924

Berliner ends his experiments in early 1924 (Munroe, 1929)

Fall 1926

Bureau of Standards established research station on perfecting the radio beacon in the fall of 1926 (Munroe, 1929)

1928

First successful test of the reed-type beacon made during flight from New York to College Park during early 1928 (Munroe, 1929)

October 2, 1933

An advertisement in The Diamondback, a University of Maryland student newspaper, advertises passenger flights above the university for $1 (The Diamondback, 1933), which is $24.31 in 2024.

October 7, 1934

Over 500 vistors watch the College Park Airshow, which featured, among other things, a gas inflated dummy named "Windy" Hydrogen jumping from an airplane. (The Diamondback, 1934)

October 14, 1934

The first all-women air show held at the College Park featured events such as a parachute jumping contest (The Diamondback, 1934)

October 9, 1938

Peter Riedel, a German Embassy Aide, flies a plane at the College Park Airshow. (The Diamondback, 1938)

May 7, 1939

Attendees celebrate Langley Day at the College Park Airport, which honors Samuel Langley, an important figure in early aviation. (The Diamondback, 1939)

November 1939

Students at the University of Maryland start taking lessons from the Civil Aeronautics Authority at the airport, which include three 30 minutes lessons per week until they reach 35-55 hours of instruction. (The Diamondback, 1939)

October 29, 1940

"Treat the ship like you would handle a lady, gently and firmly, but masterfully. Just over the trees, now; what if the motor quit—we would certainly make a terrific splash in the lake below. Look back, nose down, turn. Come on, coordinate that stick and rudder. You boot the rudder. Look, there is the University of Maryland campus below us," said an anonymous student. (The Diamondback, 1940)

August 15, 1942

College Park Airport is closed starting August 15 because of a coastal flying ban. (The Diamondback, 1942)

October 27, 1944

An advertisement in The Diamondback promotes flying lessons by the Brinckerhoff Flying Service. (The Diamondback, 1944)

October 26, 1945

An article from The Diamondback features freshman heiress Wilma Warrington, a UMD student who flies at the College Park Airport in her free time. (The Diamondback, 1945)

1956

The Committee for the Perpetuation of the College Park Airport proposes plans to create a College Park Airport musuem. (The Diamondback, 1956)

September 28, 1957

A UMD football game features the band performing tributes to "Maryland Firsts" one of which included them playing "Come Josephine in My Flying Machine" and arranging in a plane formation. (The Diamondback, 1957)

April 29, 1957

A Piper Apache plane crashes into the UMD lacrosse practice field after taking off from the College Park Airport. (The Diamondback, 1957)

1965

A helicopter taking off from the College Park Airport monitors Washington area traffic conditions for the radio station WWDC. (The Diamondback, 1965)

May 1966

The National Aerospace Educational Memorial Center (NAEMC) is established to preserve the College Park Airport. (The Diamondback, 1966)

November 1966

NAEMC struggles to put together a $100,000 down payment on the $3 million dollar airfield. Two anonymous sources said they would contribute the down payment if the NAEMC plan to restore the airport is approved by the College Park government and Maryland National Capital Park and Planning Commission by Nov. 15. (The Diamondback, 1966)

March 1967

A airport committee created by College Park to study airport preservation begins to set up its operations. (The Diamondback, 1967)

November 28, 1967

Some College Park residents living near the airport voice opposition to NAEMC plans to improve and expand the airport on the grounds that noise pollution and danger will increase. They voiced these opinions at a city council meeting to appoint members to the Airport Authority. (The Diamondback, 1967)

October 1969

College Park Airport preservation is key topic at mayoral debate, where most candidates oppose a proposal to sell the airport to Direct Airlines, with the opinion that it would expand both the airport and its noise levels. (The Diamondback, 1969)

January 1972

"Then the pilot points out the College Park airport, said to be one of the area's worst," quote in an article from The Diamondback describing a flight. (The Diamondback, 1972)

February 1972

College Park council member St. Clair Reeves said that the airport should lose its tax-exempt status because it is losing the city money it couse recive from its property tax, to the tune of $2,200 a year, which would be about $16,453.22 in 2024. (The Diamondback, 1972)

February 1973

The airport is sold to the National Capitol Park and Planning Commission for $1.5 million, or slightly over $10.6 million in 2024. (The Diamondback, 1973)

October 1973

A candidate for the College Park city council, Jack Skyloff, complains that loud noises from the airport disturb him and his neighbors. (The Diamondback, 1973)

October 1973

Airport removes flight school and air taxi service after pressure from a citizen's group. (The Diamondback, 1973)

January 1974

The airport permanently accepts several operating restrictions, such as no flight operations after 10 p.m. and no twin-engine aircraft over 2,000 pounds being allowed to use the airport. (The Diamondback, 1974)

August 1976

College Park residents oppose a proposal to lift some of the airport's operating restrictions, such as lifting the ban on helicopters and multi-engine planes. (The Diamondback, 1976)

October 15, 1977

A pilot is arrested for violating the 10 p.m. flying curfew. (The Diamondback, 1977)

October 1977

The College Park Airport is designated a national historical site. (The Diamondback, 1977)

1978

A University of Maryland student, Walt Starling, flys from the College Park Airport to monitor traffic conditions for the WASH radio station. (The Diamondback, 1978)

September 1981

Gary Doyle, a College Park resident, takes his 5-year-old son on an airplane ride at the College Park Airport open house.
"Doyle’s son. whose brown eyes beamed after the plane landed, had seen enough for one day. Although Jason says the trip did not scare him, he shook his head dramatically when another ride was mentioned." (The Diamondback, 1981)

September 11, 1982

The College Park Airport Museum opens. (The Diamondback, 1982)

September 1985

Almost 20,000 people attend the College Park Airport open house. (The Diamondback, 1985)

June 14, 1986

The Prince George’s County Festival of Flight is celebrated at the College Park Airport. (The Diamondback, 1986)
